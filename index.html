Structure

The structure of a rocket can be defined as the body tube, motor mounts as well as mounting of any other components inside the rocket. More simply put, the structure is essentially the flusk/tower/cylinder with a cone on top that is visible along with the objects(mounts) used to hold together the fuel tanks, the engine(s) etc. The structure of the rocket has to be strong but lightweight as well. Strong, to withstand the forces associated with overcoming the pull of gravity(think about it, when you jump you cant stay airborn for more than a second or so, how is a tower weighing more than a dosen elephants going to do it). Lightweight to reduce the amount of force required to get off the ground. Gravity, again!

materials commonly used to build rocket bodies include:

  • aluminum
  • titanium
Payload

This refers to what is being transported via the rocket, also, do not be deceived by a rockets size. More often than not, only a fraction of the structure will reach out of space as rockets tend to this thing know as "stage separation" where engine, empty fuel tanks etc are shed to reduce mass. The phrase "Payload Capacity" refers to how much a rocket can actually carry, whether it be people, satellites, equipment etc.
